Neck Pain

Common symptoms

  • Neck pain or stiffness, especially with turning your head
  • Pain that spreads into the shoulders or arms
  • Headaches that start at the base of the skull

What it is

The neck (cervical spine) has the same discs and small joints as the rest of your spine — and they can wear down or become irritated the same way. That can cause pain locally or send it into your shoulders and arms.

How we may help

After a careful evaluation of where your neck pain is coming from, minimally invasive cervical options — including tissue-sparing facet stabilization — may be part of your care plan when appropriate.

SI Joint Pain

Common symptoms

  • Pain in the low back, buttock, hip, or groin
  • Pain that gets worse with sitting, standing up, or climbing stairs
  • A feeling that your leg might give way

What it is

The sacroiliac (SI) joint connects your spine to your pelvis. When this joint becomes irritated or unstable, it can cause pain that is often mistaken for other back problems.

How we may help

We offer a minimally invasive SI joint fusion using an inferior posterior approach — placed through a small opening from behind, designed to stabilize the joint while preserving the surrounding muscles and tissue.

Facet Joint Pain

Common symptoms

  • Back pain that gets worse when you lean back or twist
  • Stiffness, especially in the morning
  • Tenderness over the spine

What it is

Facet joints are the small joints that connect the bones of your spine and let it bend and twist. Like any joint, they can wear down over time and become a source of ongoing pain.

How we may help

We offer minimally invasive facet fusion options designed to stabilize the painful joint through a very small incision, sparing the surrounding tissue.

Chronic Low Back Pain

Common symptoms

  • Back pain lasting longer than three months
  • Pain that limits work, sleep, or activities you enjoy
  • Pain that hasn't improved with rest, medication, or therapy

What it is

Chronic low back pain can come from several sources — joints, discs, or the structures around them. Finding the true source is the most important step toward treating it.

How we may help

Our team works with physicians who evaluate where your pain is coming from, then match you with the treatment pathway that may be appropriate — including minimally invasive options when suitable.

Discogenic Pain

Common symptoms

  • Deep, aching pain in the low back
  • Pain that gets worse with sitting or bending forward
  • Pain that may spread into the buttock or leg

What it is

Between each bone in your spine is a cushion called a disc. When a disc is damaged or worn, it can become a source of pain on its own — this is called discogenic pain.

How we may help

Depending on your evaluation, minimally invasive treatment options may help address disc-related pain while limiting disruption to healthy tissue.

Grade I Spondylolisthesis

Common symptoms

  • Low back pain that worsens with standing or walking
  • Pain, tingling, or weakness that travels into the legs
  • Tight hamstrings

What it is

Spondylolisthesis means one of the bones in your spine has slipped slightly forward over the one below it. "Grade I" means the slip is small — but it can still cause real pain.

How we may help

Minimally invasive stabilization options may help address the slipped segment while sparing muscles and other healthy anatomy.

Degenerative Spine Conditions

Common symptoms

  • Gradually worsening back or neck pain over years
  • Stiffness and reduced flexibility
  • Pain that flares with activity

What it is

"Degenerative" simply means wear-and-tear. Over time, discs and joints in the spine naturally age — and for some people, that process becomes painful.

How we may help

Because degeneration can affect different parts of the spine, treatment starts with a careful evaluation. When appropriate, tissue-sparing minimally invasive options may be part of your care plan.

What is the inferior posterior approach?

It's a way of reaching the SI joint from below and behind, through a small opening, following the natural path of the joint itself. Because the instruments travel along the joint rather than through the muscle beside it, the technique is designed to preserve the surrounding tissues while the joint is stabilized. Your physician can explain whether this approach is appropriate for your anatomy and condition.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

Am I a candidate?
Only a physician can determine that after reviewing your history, an exam, and imaging. Candidacy usually depends on where your pain is coming from and whether conservative care (like therapy, medication, or injections) has already been tried. Filling out our contact form is the easiest first step.
What is minimally invasive spine surgery?
It's surgery done through very small openings using specialized instruments, instead of the long incisions used in traditional open surgery. The goal is to treat the problem while disturbing as little of the surrounding muscle and tissue as possible.
What is SI joint dysfunction?
The sacroiliac (SI) joint connects your spine to your pelvis. When it becomes irritated, inflamed, or unstable, it can cause pain in the low back, buttock, hip, or groin — often mistaken for other back problems.
What is facet-mediated pain?
Facet joints are small joints connecting the bones of your spine. When they wear down or become inflamed, they can cause back pain — typically worse when leaning back or twisting. That's facet-mediated pain.
Will I need surgery?
Not necessarily. Many people improve with non-surgical care. Surgery is generally considered when conservative treatments haven't provided enough relief and a clear source of pain has been identified. Your physician will walk you through the options.
How long is recovery?
It varies by procedure and by person. Minimally invasive techniques are generally associated with shorter recovery than open surgery, and some procedures are done as outpatient visits — but your physician will give you expectations specific to your situation.
What should I expect during my consultation?
Expect a conversation about your symptoms and history, a physical exam, and possibly imaging review. You should leave understanding what's likely causing your pain and what your options are — surgical and non-surgical. Bring your questions; a good consultation answers them.
