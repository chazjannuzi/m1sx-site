Why minimally invasive

Smaller incisions. Faster recovery. Better outcomes.

The goal of minimally invasive spine (MIS) surgery is to stabilize the vertebral bones and spinal joints and relieve pressure on the spinal nerves — often caused by spinal instability, bone spurs, herniated discs, scoliosis, or spinal tumors. Compared to open procedures, reduced trauma to muscles and soft tissue means:

Smaller incisions

Better cosmetic results from incisions sometimes as small as several millimeters.

Less blood loss

Reduced surgical blood loss compared to open spine procedures.

Muscle preservation

Reduced risk of muscle damage — less or no cutting of muscle required.

Lower infection risk

Reduced risk of infection and less postoperative pain.

Faster recovery

Quicker return to daily life, with less rehabilitation required.

Fewer pain meds

Diminished reliance on pain medications after surgery.

Some MIS surgeries are performed as outpatient procedures using only local anesthesia — reducing the risk of an adverse reaction to general anesthesia.

Understanding the risks — an honest word
As with any surgical procedure, no matter how minimal, there are certain risks, including but not limited to:
  • Possible adverse reaction to the anesthetic
  • Unexpected blood loss during the procedure
  • Localized infections, no matter how small the incision area
And, though uncommon, there is always a small chance that the initial MIS surgery cannot be completed — requiring either a second procedure or full open surgery. Always discuss risks and benefits with your surgeon.
Common MIS approaches

Procedures, in plain language.

A few of the most common minimally invasive spine procedures and what they do.

Decompression

Relieves pressure and pain caused by bone or disc material impinging on the spinal cord or nerves.

Discectomy

Surgical removal of part or all of an intervertebral disc to relieve pressure on a nerve root or the spinal cord.

Foraminotomy

Enlarges the bony opening where a nerve root exits the spinal canal, creating more space for the nerve.

Laminectomy

Removes the rear part of a vertebra to access the spinal cord or nerve roots and relieve pressure.

Laminotomy

Opens just part of the lamina — typically the symptomatic side — to relieve nerve root pressure.

Medial facetectomy

Removes part of the facet joint to increase space within the spinal canal.
